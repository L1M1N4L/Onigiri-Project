Introduction to Hiragana

Understanding Hiragana

Hiragana serves as the fundamental Japanese alphabet. Unlike the English alphabet, each character symbolizes a distinct syllable sound.

With a total of 46 characters, Hiragana is organized in a commonly recognized sequence:

Please take note that in vertical Japanese script, the reading sequence progresses from right to left. The first column introduces the five fundamental vowel sounds in Japanese. Remaining characters consist of a consonant sound paired with one of these vowel sounds, except for the "N" or ん character.

Understanding Dakuten

The "dakuten" is a diacritic mark used in Japanese writing to modify the pronunciation of specific characters.

It appears as a small symbol resembling a double quotation mark ( ゛ ) and is placed above or next to a Hiragana or Katakana character.

When added to certain characters, such as か (ka), き (ki), く (ku), け (ke), and こ (ko) in the Hiragana script:

  • か (ka) becomes が (ga).
  • き (ki) becomes ぎ (gi).
  • く (ku) becomes ぐ (gu).
  • け (ke) becomes げ (ge).
  • こ (ko) becomes ご (go).

The dakuten changes the sound of unvoiced consonants to voiced consonants, altering the pronunciation of these characters.

Understanding Handakuten

The "handakuten" is a diacritic mark used in Japanese writing to modify the pronunciation of specific characters.

It appears as a small symbol resembling a degree sign ( ゜ ) and is placed above or next to a Hiragana or Katakana character.

When added to certain characters, such as は (ha), ひ (hi), ふ (fu), へ (he), and ほ (ho) in the Hiragana script:

  • は (ha) becomes ぱ (pa).
  • ひ (hi) becomes ぴ (pi).
  • ふ (fu) becomes ぷ (pu).
  • へ (he) becomes ぺ (pe).
  • ほ (ho) becomes ぽ (po).
